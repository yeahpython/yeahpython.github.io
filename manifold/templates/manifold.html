Ideas

Here are some things that I think can benefit from being explained interactively.
  • column space and null space (row space would be interesting, too!):
    • The scene would show a matrix, the domain (1D, 2D, or 3D) and the range (1D, 2D, or 3D), with the standard basis shown in the plot of the domain and the vectors that correspond to the columns of the matrix shown in the plot of the range.
    • Entries of the matrix can be modified directly, but you can also modify the column vectors directly in the plot of the range. To help people remember that these vectors in the range are the images of unit vectors, I would highlight the entries of the matrix that change when you move the column vectors around.
    • With snapping, you can let the user construct a matrix with a nontrival null space, and show how a space is compressed to a point everywhere. This may help to explain the rank-nullity theorem.
    • A separate animation showing the algorithm for solving matrices will help convey that the algorithm messes with the column space but it doesn't change the null space.
  • Partial derivatives: I like this image from www.vias.org
  • the Jacobian operator: I like this wikipedia image, although it's not really a Jacobian. Here's what we have: Discussion of map projection controversies might also help.
    • The scene would show the domain of the function, the range of the function, a plot of the unit basis and plot of the column vectors of the Jacobian of the function.
    • The user controls a 3D cursor, checking out how the Jacobian changes at every point of the function
    • By associating the unit basis with the range plot and the column vectors with the domain plot, it becomes clearer that the Jacobian is a tool for linear approximations.
  • The chain rule
    • So we have f:A->B and g:B->C. The scene shows how g composed f moves things from A to B, then to C. We also show linear approximations at x, f(x) and g(f(x)).
    • The user moves around a 3D cursor, upon which it gradually becomes clear that why we're multiplying these particular linear approximations.
    • Extension: Linking up n function to show why this is a chain
  • curl: Not totally sure how to explain curl. It seems really scary, but it really is a simple geometric thing. I like this visualization of a vector field. Here are the most annoying things about them:
    • Why is it that this magic formula of a vector of sums of partial derivatives of vector fields is the right thing to use?
    • How come you can just combine curls in the XY, YZ and ZX planes into a vector?
  • div: I don't really think interactivity will benefit this
  • grad: I don't really think interactivity will benefit this
  • differential forms: I think the pullback operator has a super nice visual interpretation, and animations should help this a lot. The question of why you need to care about determinants of jacobians will also probably be explained here. Maybe something about the Robinson projection will help.
